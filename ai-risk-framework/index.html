AI Risk Assessment Framework:
Customer Claims Chatbot Deployment

A structured pre-deployment risk assessment of an AI-powered chatbot handling motor insurance claims queries for UK retail customers. Evaluates risks across bias, explainability, data privacy, FCA Consumer Duty, and operational resilience — with a scored risk register and governance recommendations.

Author: Ankit Singh
Use Case: AI Claims Chatbot — UK Motor Insurance
Regulatory Scope: FCA Consumer Duty · UK GDPR · ICO · Equality Act 2010
Risk Domains: 6 assessed
Risks Identified: 12 (3 Critical · 4 High · 3 Medium · 2 Low)
Date: April 2026

Executive Summary

This framework assesses the risks of deploying an AI-powered chatbot to handle first-notice-of-loss (FNOL) and claims status queries for UK motor insurance customers. The chatbot would handle an estimated 60–70% of inbound claims interactions, reducing operational costs while maintaining FCA-compliant customer outcomes.

The assessment identifies 12 risks across 6 domains. Three risks are rated Critical and require mandatory controls before deployment: AI bias in claims handling decisions, inadequate GDPR consent mechanisms, and failure to meet FCA Consumer Duty obligations on consumer understanding. Four High risks require mitigation plans with defined owners and timelines.

Overall deployment readiness rating: CONDITIONAL — deployment is viable subject to implementation of the 9 priority controls outlined in this framework.

Risk Scoring Methodology

Each risk is scored on two dimensions — Likelihood (1–5) and Impact (1–5) — producing a Risk Score (1–25). Scores are mapped to four severity tiers. Residual risk is assessed after proposed controls are applied.

Risk Register

12 risks identified across 6 domains. Each entry includes a description, likelihood and impact scores, regulatory mapping, proposed controls, and residual risk rating after controls are applied.

R-01

AI bias in claims handling — discriminatory outcomes for protected characteristics

CRITICAL · Score 20/25
Risk description

The AI model may produce systematically different claims outcomes for customers based on age, disability status, or postcode — proxies for protected characteristics under the Equality Act 2010. Training data from historical claims may encode past human biases.

Likelihood · Impact · Score

Likelihood: 4/5 — LLMs trained on historical data routinely reproduce existing biases without explicit bias detection and remediation.

Impact: 5/5 — FCA enforcement action, Equality Act liability, and significant reputational harm.

Required controls
Pre-deployment bias audit across protected characteristics Ongoing fairness monitoring (monthly) Diverse test dataset including edge cases Human review for all declined/disputed claims Explainability logs retained for audit
Regulatory mapping: FCA Consumer Duty — Consumer Outcomes Equality Act 2010 — s.19 Indirect Discrimination FCA PS22/9 — Products & Services Outcome
Residual risk after controls: Medium — 8/25
R-02

GDPR — inadequate lawful basis and consent for AI processing of claims data

CRITICAL · Score 20/25
Risk description

The chatbot will process sensitive personal data (health information in injury claims, financial data, vehicle history). UK GDPR Article 9 requires explicit consent or another lawful basis for special category data. Failure to establish a clear lawful basis before deployment constitutes a data breach.

Likelihood · Impact · Score

Likelihood: 4/5 — Many AI deployments proceed without adequate privacy impact assessments.

Impact: 5/5 — ICO enforcement, fines up to £17.5m or 4% global turnover, plus civil liability.

Required controls
Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) mandatory pre-deployment Explicit Article 9 lawful basis documented Privacy notice updated to cover AI processing Data minimisation — chatbot collects only necessary data Retention schedule defined for AI-generated interaction logs
Regulatory mapping: UK GDPR Art. 6 — Lawful basis UK GDPR Art. 9 — Special category data UK GDPR Art. 35 — DPIA requirement ICO AI Auditing Framework
Residual risk after controls: Medium — 8/25
R-03

FCA Consumer Duty — failure to ensure customer understanding of AI-generated guidance

CRITICAL · Score 16/25
Risk description

Consumer Duty requires firms to ensure customers understand the information provided and can make informed decisions. If the chatbot provides unclear, incomplete, or misleading claims guidance, customers may act to their detriment — a direct breach of the Consumer Understanding outcome.

Likelihood · Impact · Score

Likelihood: 4/5 — LLMs hallucinate or produce ambiguous outputs, especially in complex claims scenarios.

Impact: 4/5 — FCA supervisory action, customer redress obligations, reputational harm.

Required controls
Plain English testing of all chatbot outputs Mandatory human escalation for complex or high-value claims Explicit disclosure: "You are talking to an AI" Output confidence thresholds — low-confidence responses trigger escalation Regular QA sampling of live chatbot interactions
Regulatory mapping: FCA Consumer Duty — Consumer Understanding Outcome FCA Consumer Duty — Consumer Support Outcome FCA PRIN 12 — Act to deliver good outcomes
Residual risk after controls: Medium — 9/25
R-04

Explainability failure — AI cannot justify claims decisions to customers or regulators

HIGH · Score 15/25
Risk description

Customers have a right under UK GDPR to request meaningful information about automated decisions that significantly affect them. If the chatbot cannot produce explainable, auditable reasoning for claims outcomes, the firm cannot fulfil this obligation or defend decisions to the FCA.

Likelihood · Impact · Score

Likelihood: 3/5 — Modern LLMs are not inherently explainable; explainability tools are available but require deliberate implementation.

Impact: 5/5 — Regulatory enforcement, inability to defend complaints, ICO audit risk.

Required controls
Explainability layer (e.g. LIME/SHAP or chain-of-thought logging) Decision audit trail retained for minimum 5 years Human review available on request for any AI-influenced outcome Model card documenting training data, limitations, and intended scope
Regulatory mapping: UK GDPR Art. 22 — Automated decision-making ICO Explaining AI Decisions guidance FCA Consumer Duty — Consumer Support Outcome
Residual risk after controls: Medium — 9/25
R-05

Operational resilience — chatbot failure causes claims handling outage

HIGH · Score 12/25
Risk description

If the chatbot handles the majority of claims queries and experiences a model failure, API outage, or adversarial prompt attack, customer access to claims support is disrupted. This is particularly acute for vulnerable customers requiring urgent support following an accident.

Likelihood · Impact · Score

Likelihood: 3/5 — AI systems experience outages; adversarial inputs are a known attack vector for LLMs.

Impact: 4/5 — Customer harm, FCA operational resilience breach, reputational damage.

Required controls
Fallback routing to human agents on failure Maximum tolerable disruption period defined (FCA OpRes) Adversarial prompt testing pre-deployment SLA monitoring with automated alerting
Regulatory mapping: FCA Operational Resilience PS21/3 FCA Consumer Duty — Consumer Support Outcome
Residual risk after controls: Low — 4/25
R-06

Vulnerable customer identification — AI fails to detect distress or vulnerability signals

HIGH · Score 12/25
Risk description

FCA Consumer Duty and FCA guidance on vulnerable customers (FG21/1) require firms to identify and appropriately support customers in vulnerable circumstances. An AI chatbot may fail to detect signals of distress, mental health challenges, or bereavement — resulting in inadequate support and potential harm.

Likelihood · Impact · Score

Likelihood: 4/5 — Detecting nuanced vulnerability signals in text is a known limitation of LLMs without specific fine-tuning.

Impact: 3/5 — Customer harm, FCA supervisory concern, complaints and redress costs.

Required controls
Vulnerability detection classifier layer (keyword + sentiment) Auto-escalation to human agent on vulnerability flag Staff training on handling AI-escalated vulnerable cases Quarterly review of escalation accuracy rates
Regulatory mapping: FCA FG21/1 — Vulnerable Customers FCA Consumer Duty — Consumer Support Outcome
Residual risk after controls: Medium — 6/25
R-07

Third-party AI vendor risk — limited oversight of model updates and data practices

HIGH · Score 10/25
Risk description

If the chatbot is built on a third-party LLM (e.g. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini), the firm has limited visibility into model updates, training data changes, or data processing practices. A model update could change behaviour materially without notice, creating compliance exposure.

Likelihood · Impact · Score

Likelihood: 2/5 — Manageable with contractual controls, but common in early AI vendor contracts.

Impact: 5/5 — Undetected compliance breach, data exfiltration risk, model drift.

Required controls
AI vendor due diligence questionnaire Contractual right to audit model changes Data processing agreement covering AI training data Model version pinning with change notification clauses
Regulatory mapping: UK GDPR Art. 28 — Processor obligations FCA SYSC — Outsourcing and third-party risk
Residual risk after controls: Low — 4/25
R-08

Model hallucination — chatbot provides factually incorrect claims information

MEDIUM · Score 9/25
Risk description

LLMs can generate plausible but incorrect information about policy terms, excess amounts, or claims procedures. A customer acting on incorrect AI guidance may fail to submit a valid claim or incur unexpected costs.

Likelihood · Impact · Score

Likelihood: 3/5 — Hallucination is a known LLM failure mode; retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) reduces but does not eliminate it.

Impact: 3/5 — Customer detriment, complaints, potential redress.

Required controls
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) grounding on policy documents Confidence scoring — low-confidence outputs escalated Weekly QA sampling of live chatbot outputs
Regulatory mapping: FCA Consumer Duty — Consumer Understanding Outcome
Residual risk after controls: Low — 3/25
Governance Framework — Accountability Structure

Under SM&CR, a named Senior Manager must be accountable for AI-related consumer outcomes. The following governance structure is recommended for pre- and post-deployment oversight.

First Line

Business / Product
  • AI product owner accountable for day-to-day performance
  • Operates and monitors chatbot controls
  • Reviews QA sampling outputs weekly
  • Manages human escalation routing
  • Reports incidents to second line

Second Line

Risk & Compliance
  • Owns this AI risk framework
  • Conducts monthly compliance monitoring reviews
  • Reviews DPIA and regulatory mapping annually
  • Escalates material risks to Board
  • Coordinates with FCA on AI-related queries

Third Line

Internal Audit
  • Independent annual audit of AI governance controls
  • Reviews bias testing results and methodology
  • Assesses GDPR/Consumer Duty compliance
  • Reports directly to Audit Committee
  • Benchmarks against ICO AI Auditing Framework

Priority Recommendations
  • 1
    Conduct a mandatory DPIA before any deployment activity

    A Data Protection Impact Assessment is a legal requirement under UK GDPR Art. 35 for high-risk AI processing. This must be completed, reviewed by the DPO, and documented before any live customer data is processed by the chatbot.

    Pre-deployment
  • 2
    Commission an independent AI bias audit across all protected characteristics

    Before deployment, the model must be tested for differential outcomes across age, disability, ethnicity, and other protected characteristics using a representative test dataset. Results must be documented and signed off by a named Senior Manager.

    Pre-deployment
  • 3
    Implement mandatory AI disclosure and human escalation pathways

    All customers must be clearly informed they are interacting with an AI system at the start of the conversation. A clearly accessible "speak to a human" option must be available at all times — not just on chatbot failure.

    Pre-deployment
  • 4
    Build a vulnerability detection layer with auto-escalation

    Implement a secondary classifier that monitors conversation inputs for vulnerability signals (distress language, repeated queries, references to bereavement or health issues) and automatically routes affected customers to a human agent.

    Month 1
  • 5
    Establish a QA monitoring programme for live chatbot outputs

    A minimum of 100 chatbot interactions per week should be sampled by the compliance function and reviewed against Consumer Duty standards. Findings should be reported to the AI governance committee monthly.

    Month 1
  • 6
    Assign a named SM&CR Senior Manager as AI Accountability Owner

    Under SM&CR, a specific Senior Manager must be named as accountable for AI-related consumer outcomes. This accountability should be recorded in the Statements of Responsibilities and governance map.

    Pre-deployment

This AI Risk Assessment Framework was developed independently by Ankit Singh as a portfolio project demonstrating applied skills in AI governance, regulatory compliance, and risk management within UK financial services.

The framework draws on direct professional experience in FCA-regulated insurance firms (Acorn Insurance, Direct Line Group) and applies established risk management methodologies to an AI deployment scenario — bridging traditional compliance expertise with emerging AI governance requirements.
