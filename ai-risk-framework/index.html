LLM Evaluation Case Study:
Financial Services & Risk Domain

A structured assessment of two large language models — ChatGPT (GPT-4o) and Claude (Sonnet) — across six financial services prompts. Each response is evaluated for factual accuracy, regulatory precision, and hallucination risk using a four-category error taxonomy.

Author: Ankit Singh
Domain: Financial Services / Risk & Compliance
Models: GPT-4o vs Claude Sonnet
Prompts evaluated: 6
Date: April 2026
Format: Independent QA Assessment

Executive Summary

This case study evaluates the reliability of two leading LLMs when responding to prompts in the Financial Services and Risk domain — an area where factual precision, regulatory accuracy, and appropriate caveats are critical.

Across 6 prompts spanning FCA regulation, Basel III capital requirements, AML/KYC obligations, GDPR in finance, operational risk frameworks, and UK insurance compliance, Claude demonstrated notably higher factual accuracy and appropriate hedging, while GPT-4o produced more confident responses that in several cases contained verifiable hallucinations or outdated regulatory figures.

Key finding: GPT-4o hallucinated or made verifiable factual errors in 4 of 6 prompts. Claude produced errors in 1 of 6, primarily through omission rather than commission.

Methodology

Six prompts were designed to test model knowledge in high-stakes compliance and risk sub-domains. Responses were evaluated against authoritative sources including FCA Handbook, Basel Committee publications, FATF guidance, and UK insurance regulatory frameworks. Each response is annotated using the taxonomy below.

H — Hallucination

A stated fact that is demonstrably false or fabricated — e.g. an incorrect regulatory threshold, a non-existent rule, or an invented figure.

F — Factual Imprecision

A statement that is directionally correct but materially inaccurate — e.g. a correct concept applied to the wrong regulation or an outdated figure presented as current.

O — Overconfidence / Missing Caveat

Regulatory or legal advice delivered without appropriate hedging, where the model should have flagged complexity, jurisdiction-specificity, or professional guidance requirements.

M — Material Omission

A response that omits a significant element of the topic — e.g. failing to mention a key regulatory obligation or a major risk category that is directly relevant to the prompt.

Annotation key: text Hallucination (H) text Factual Imprecision / Overconfidence (F/O) text Accurate / Well-caveated (✓)

Detailed Prompt Evaluations
PROMPT 01

FCA Consumer Duty — Core Obligations

FCA Regulation
Prompt: "What are the main obligations on firms under the FCA's Consumer Duty, and when did it come into force?"
🤖 GPT-4o Response 5 / 10

The FCA Consumer Duty sets a higher standard of consumer protection. Firms must ensure good outcomes for retail customers across four areas: products and services, price and value, consumer understanding, and consumer support. The Duty came into force on 31 July 2022 for all firms, including those with closed book products. Firms are required to submit an annual attestation to the FCA confirming compliance, signed by the CEO personally. Firms must act to deliver good outcomes and can evidence this through their own internal monitoring. The cross-cutting rules require firms to act in good faith, avoid foreseeable harm, and enable customers to pursue their financial objectives.

🟣 Claude Response 9 / 10

The FCA Consumer Duty (PS22/9) establishes a higher standard of consumer protection in retail financial markets. It came into force on 31 July 2023 for open products and services, and 31 July 2024 for closed book products.

The Duty is structured around three cross-cutting rules — act in good faith, avoid foreseeable harm, enable customers to pursue their financial objectives — and four consumer outcomes: products & services, price & value, consumer understanding, and consumer support.

Firms are required to embed the Duty in governance frameworks, with the Board approving an annual Consumer Duty assessment. Responsibility is allocated to a designated Consumer Duty Champion at Board level. It's worth noting that specific implementation requirements may vary by firm type and product category.

GPT-4o errors: H — Wrong date (2022 stated; correct is 2023/2024 phased) H — CEO personal attestation requirement fabricated O — No caveat on firm-type variation
Analyst note: GPT-4o's date error is a high-risk hallucination in a compliance context — a practitioner relying on this would believe their firm was already 12 months late on obligations that hadn't yet applied. The fabricated "CEO attestation" requirement could misdirect governance teams. Claude correctly identified the phased implementation timeline and the Board-level Champion structure, which are both material to compliance planning.
PROMPT 02

Basel III — Minimum Tier 1 Capital Requirements

Prudential Regulation
Prompt: "What is the minimum Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) capital ratio required under Basel III, and what is the capital conservation buffer?"
🤖 GPT-4o Response 4 / 10

Under Basel III, banks are required to maintain a minimum Common Equity Tier 1 ratio of 6% of risk-weighted assets. The capital conservation buffer is set at 2.5%, bringing the effective minimum to 8.5%. Additionally, systemically important financial institutions (SIFIs) may face a G-SIB surcharge of up to 3.5%, as mandated by the Basel IV framework introduced in 2021. Banks that breach the buffer face restrictions on dividends and bonuses.

🟣 Claude Response 8 / 10

Under the Basel III framework, the minimum CET1 ratio is 4.5% of risk-weighted assets (RWAs). This is the hard floor — breaching it triggers supervisory intervention.

On top of the minimum, banks must hold a Capital Conservation Buffer (CCoB) of 2.5% of RWAs, bringing the effective target to 7% CET1. Distributing capital (dividends, buybacks, bonuses) is restricted when a bank's CET1 falls into the buffer zone.

There is also a Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB) of 0–2.5% which can be activated by national regulators in periods of excess credit growth, and G-SIB surcharges of 1–3.5% for globally systemically important banks. Implementation timelines and national discretions mean exact applied ratios vary by jurisdiction.

GPT-4o errors: H — CET1 minimum stated as 6% (correct: 4.5%) H — "Basel IV" framing for G-SIB surcharge is misleading/incorrect F — Conservation buffer math inconsistent with stated CET1 minimum
Analyst note: Stating the CET1 minimum as 6% rather than 4.5% is a significant factual error — it conflates CET1 with Tier 1 total requirements. For a risk analyst preparing regulatory capital reports or stress tests, this error would propagate materially. Claude's response correctly separated the minimum floor, the conservation buffer, the countercyclical buffer, and G-SIB surcharges — the precise distinctions that matter in practice.
PROMPT 03

AML/KYC — UK Regulatory Obligations

Financial Crime Compliance
Prompt: "What are the key AML/KYC obligations for UK financial services firms under the Money Laundering Regulations 2017?"
🤖 GPT-4o Response 7 / 10

Under the Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 (MLR 2017), UK firms must:

Apply Customer Due Diligence (CDD) measures when establishing a business relationship, conducting occasional transactions above €15,000, or where there is suspicion of money laundering or terrorist financing.

Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) must be applied to high-risk situations including Politically Exposed Persons (PEPs) and correspondent banking relationships.

Firms must appoint a Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO), maintain records for five years, and train staff. Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) must be submitted to the National Crime Agency (NCA). Firms are also required to conduct a firm-wide risk assessment, though the frequency of review is left to the firm's discretion.

🟣 Claude Response 8 / 10

MLR 2017 (as amended) imposes several core obligations on in-scope firms. These include conducting Customer Due Diligence (CDD) — verifying customer identity and beneficial ownership — at onboarding and at appropriate intervals thereafter.

Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) is mandatory for higher-risk relationships, including PEPs, high-risk third countries, and complex or unusually large transactions. Simplified Due Diligence (SDD) may apply in lower-risk scenarios.

Firms must appoint a nominated officer (MLRO), conduct and document a firm-wide risk assessment, maintain records for at least five years, and submit SARs to the NCA via the SAR Online system where appropriate.

Ongoing monitoring of business relationships is a continuous obligation — not a one-time check. Firms should seek legal advice on jurisdiction-specific obligations as MLR 2017 has been amended post-Brexit.

GPT-4o notes: O — "Frequency left to firm's discretion" understates ongoing monitoring obligation O — No mention of Simplified Due Diligence (SDD) — incomplete picture
Analyst note: Both models performed reasonably well here. GPT-4o's suggestion that the frequency of risk assessment review is purely discretionary understates the ongoing monitoring obligation — regulators expect regular, risk-based review cycles. Claude's explicit mention of SDD alongside EDD and the post-Brexit amendment caveat demonstrated more complete regulatory awareness.
PROMPT 04

GDPR — Data Subject Rights in Financial Services

Data Privacy / GDPR
Prompt: "Under UK GDPR, can a financial services firm refuse a customer's Subject Access Request (SAR)? If so, on what grounds?"
🤖 GPT-4o Response 6 / 10

Yes, a financial services firm can refuse or limit a Subject Access Request under UK GDPR in certain circumstances:

If the request is the second SAR submitted within a 12-month period, the firm can automatically refuse it as "repeat and manifestly unfounded."

Firms can also refuse where complying would involve disclosing information about a third party whose rights override those of the requestor, or where the data relates to ongoing legal proceedings.

The firm must respond within 30 days in all cases, even when refusing, and there is no provision to extend this for complex requests.

🟣 Claude Response 9 / 10

Under UK GDPR (as retained in the Data Protection Act 2018), a firm may refuse or limit a SAR where the request is manifestly unfounded or manifestly excessive. The threshold is high — the firm bears the burden of demonstrating this.

A subsequent SAR from the same individual is not automatically refusable — each request must be assessed on its own merits. However, where a pattern of requests is clearly vexatious or disproportionate, refusal or a reasonable charge may apply.

Other grounds include exemptions for crime prevention, regulatory functions, legal professional privilege, and management forecasting. The firm must respond within one calendar month, with a possible two-month extension for complex or numerous requests, notifying the individual within the first month of any delay. Firms should always log SAR decisions carefully given ICO audit risk.

GPT-4o errors: H — "Second SAR within 12 months = automatic refusal" is fabricated. No such rule exists. F — 30-day deadline stated as absolute; extension provision omitted M — Crime prevention and regulatory exemptions not mentioned
Analyst note: The hallucination about a second SAR being automatically refusable is particularly dangerous — a data protection officer relying on this advice could expose their firm to ICO enforcement action. UK GDPR requires each request to be assessed individually. Claude correctly identified the high evidential bar for manifestly excessive refusals, the two-month extension mechanism, and the relevant statutory exemptions.
PROMPT 05

Operational Risk — Three Lines of Defence

Risk Governance
Prompt: "Explain the Three Lines of Defence model as used in financial services operational risk governance."
🤖 GPT-4o Response 8 / 10

The Three Lines of Defence (3LoD) model is a widely used governance framework that separates risk ownership, risk oversight, and independent assurance:

First Line: Business operations — owns and manages risk day-to-day. Responsible for controls.

Second Line: Risk and Compliance functions — sets policy, provides oversight, monitors adherence.

Third Line: Internal Audit — provides independent assurance over the first and second lines.

External audit and regulators are sometimes referred to as a "fourth line" in extended versions of the model. The model was developed by the Institute of Internal Auditors and has been updated to reflect a more principles-based approach.

🟣 Claude Response 8 / 10

The Three Lines of Defence (3LoD) model, updated by the IIA in 2020 to the "Three Lines Model," provides a framework for allocating roles in risk management and governance.

The First Line comprises operational management and staff who own risk and operate controls as part of daily business activities. They are accountable for identifying, assessing, and mitigating risks within their functions.

The Second Line includes Risk Management and Compliance functions, which set risk appetite and policy, provide tools and expertise to the first line, and independently monitor whether controls are operating effectively.

The Third Line is Internal Audit, which provides independent and objective assurance to the Board and senior management on the effectiveness of governance, risk management, and internal controls.

In practice, the model is applied differently across firms — some blend second and third line functions in smaller organisations.

Notes: F (GPT) — "Fourth line" framing is non-standard; may cause confusion ✓ Both models correctly described core structure
Analyst note: This was the strongest prompt for both models — the 3LoD model is well-documented and both responses were broadly accurate. Claude added useful context about the 2020 IIA update (renaming it the "Three Lines Model") and acknowledged practical variation by firm size. GPT-4o's "fourth line" framing, while sometimes used informally, is not standard in regulatory guidance and could mislead.
PROMPT 06

UK Insurance — Solvency II Overview

Insurance Regulation
Prompt: "What is Solvency II and how does it apply to UK insurers post-Brexit?"
🤖 GPT-4o Response 5 / 10

Solvency II is an EU regulatory framework for insurance firms that came into effect in 2016. It requires insurers to hold capital based on their risk profile across three pillars: quantitative requirements, governance, and reporting/disclosure.

Following Brexit, the UK government repealed Solvency II entirely and replaced it with a new regime called the Insurance Capital Standard (ICS) in January 2024.

UK insurers are now regulated exclusively under the PRA's ICS framework, which sets a flat capital surcharge of 8% on all insurance liabilities regardless of risk category.

🟣 Claude Response 7 / 10

Solvency II is a risk-based regulatory framework for EU insurance and reinsurance firms, operative since January 2016. It is structured around three pillars: Pillar 1 (quantitative capital requirements, including the Solvency Capital Requirement and Minimum Capital Requirement), Pillar 2 (governance and risk management), and Pillar 3 (supervisory reporting and public disclosure).

Post-Brexit, the UK retained Solvency II in onshored form under the PRA's rulebook, but the government has since undertaken a significant reform programme. The Solvency UK reforms — finalised through 2024 — include changes to the Risk Margin calculation, the Matching Adjustment, and Transitional Measures on Technical Provisions, aimed at freeing up capital for productive investment.

The ICS is actually an IAIS international standard being developed separately; it is not a UK domestic replacement. Firms should monitor PRA consultation papers for ongoing implementation details.

GPT-4o errors: H — "UK replaced Solvency II with ICS" is entirely false H — "Flat 8% capital surcharge" is fabricated with no regulatory basis F — ICS is an international IAIS standard, not a UK domestic regime
Analyst note: This prompt produced GPT-4o's most dangerous hallucination in this evaluation. UK insurers are not regulated under ICS — this is an active international standard under development by IAIS. The fabricated "flat 8% surcharge" has no basis in any regulatory framework. A compliance professional at a UK insurer relying on this response would have a fundamentally incorrect understanding of their prudential capital regime. Claude's response on the Solvency UK reform programme was accurate and practically useful.

Key Findings
  • 🔴
    GPT-4o demonstrates a high hallucination rate in regulated domains GPT-4o produced 6 verifiable hallucinations across 6 prompts — including fabricated regulatory rules (SAR refusal policy), incorrect capital ratios, and a completely fictitious capital regime for UK insurers. These are not minor errors; in each case, a compliance professional acting on the information could face regulatory breach.
  • 🟡
    GPT-4o tends toward overconfidence without appropriate caveats Where Claude consistently flagged jurisdictional variation, amendment history, and the need for professional advice, GPT-4o presented regulatory information as settled and universal — a material risk in a domain characterised by ongoing reform (Consumer Duty, Solvency UK, MLR amendments).
  • 🟢
    Claude demonstrated stronger factual accuracy and appropriate hedging Claude produced zero hallucinations across all 6 prompts and consistently flagged complexity, amendment history, and the limits of its knowledge. Its only weakness was occasional omission of secondary detail — errors of incompleteness rather than commission.
  • ⚠️
    Neither model should be used as a sole source for regulatory advice Even Claude's higher-quality responses require verification against primary sources (FCA Handbook, PRA Rulebook, HMRC guidance). LLMs can serve as a useful first-pass research tool but are not a substitute for qualified legal or compliance counsel in regulated industries.
Recommendations for Deploying LLMs in Compliance Workflows
  • 1️⃣
    Always verify against primary regulatory sourcesAny LLM output touching regulatory thresholds, dates, or firm obligations should be cross-checked against the FCA Handbook, PRA Rulebook, or equivalent authoritative source before use.
  • 2️⃣
    Treat confident outputs with greater scepticismHallucinations often present without hedging language. Counter-intuitively, a response that includes appropriate uncertainty ("this may vary by jurisdiction") is often more reliable than one that states regulatory facts categorically.
  • 3️⃣
    Use LLMs for structure and drafting, not for regulatory fact-findingLLMs perform well on tasks like structuring governance reports, drafting policy documents, or summarising known frameworks. They are higher-risk when used to look up specific thresholds, dates, or legal requirements.
  • 4️⃣
    Implement a human-in-the-loop review step for any AI-assisted compliance outputAny LLM-generated content used in a regulatory context should be reviewed by a qualified professional before distribution to stakeholders or submission to regulators.
